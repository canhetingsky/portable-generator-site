## How Many Watts Do I Need for a Portable Generator?

One of the most common mistakes people make when buying a portable generator is choosing the wrong size. In most cases, the problem is not the generator itself ‚Äî it is a misunderstanding of **how many watts are actually required**.

Many users assume that estimating generator power is as simple as adding up appliance wattages. In reality, generator sizing involves several factors, including starting power, load behavior, and how appliances interact when running at the same time.

Knowing how many watts you need is the **single most important step** when choosing a portable generator.

If the generator is too small, it may overload, shut down, or damage equipment.

## Understanding Generator Watts: Running vs Starting Watts

In generator specifications, watts describe how much electrical power the generator can supply. However, not all watts are the same, and misunderstanding this distinction leads to most sizing errors.

Therefore, before calculating generator size, it‚Äôs essential to understand two types of power demand.

### Running Watts (Continuous Power)

Running watts (also called rated watts) represent the amount of power an appliance needs to operate continuously once it is already running.

Examples:

- Refrigerator: ~600‚Äì800W
- Laptop: ~50‚Äì100W
- Lights: ~10‚Äì60W each

---

### Starting Watts (Surge Power)

Some devices require a **brief surge of extra power** to start.

These include:

- Refrigerators
- Freezers
- Air conditioners
- Well pumps
- Power tools

Starting watts can be **2‚Äì3 times higher** than running watts but only last a few seconds.

A generator must handle **both** running and starting watts.

## Portable Generator Wattage Calculator (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: List Everything You Want to Power

Write down all devices you plan to run **at the same time**.

Example list:

- Refrigerator
- Microwave
- Wi-Fi router
- Laptop
- LED lights
- Phone chargers

Only include items that will operate simultaneously.

---

### Step 2: Find Running and Starting Watts

Use appliance labels, manufacturer manuals, or reliable averages.

| Device | Running Watts | Starting Watts |
|------|--------------|----------------|
| Refrigerator | 700W | 2,200W |
| Microwave | 1,000W | 1,000W |
| Wi-Fi Router | 20W | 20W |
| Laptop | 100W | 100W |

---

### Step 3: Add Running Watts

Add all running watts together:

700 + 1,000 + 20 + 100 + 300 = **2,120W**

---

### Step 4: Add the Highest Starting Watt

Add **only the single highest starting watt**, not all of them.

Highest starting watt in this example: **2,200W (refrigerator)**

Total generator wattage needed:

2,120W + 2,200W = **4,320W**

---

### Step 5: Add Safety Headroom (Generator Size)

To avoid overload and improve longevity, add **20‚Äì25% extra capacity**.

Recommended generator size:

## Generator Size Calculator Summary

To summarize the sizing process:

1. **Add all running watts** of the appliances you plan to use simultaneously.
2. **Add the single highest starting surge** (do not add starting watts for all devices).
3. **Add a 20‚Äì25% safety buffer** to protect your generator from overload.

Follow this industry-standard formula:

$$Required\ Capacity = (\sum Running\ Watts + \max Starting\ Surge) \times 1.2$$

**Where:**

- $\sum \text{Running Watts}$: Total continuous power required for all devices.
- $\max \text{Starting Surge}$: The extra power needed for your most demanding motor, such as a refrigerator or AC.

This process functions as a **manual generator size calculator** and produces reliable results for most home and emergency scenarios.
